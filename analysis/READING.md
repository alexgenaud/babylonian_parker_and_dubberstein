# Reading list and claims map

What must be read before publishing, and the exact question each source
answers. The novelty of our findings hangs on items 1-2; the rest are
context, method checks, or access routes.

## Essential - novelty gatekeepers

1. **Britton, J. P. 2007.** "Calendars, Intercalations and Year-Lengths
   in Mesopotamian Astronomy." In J. M. Steele (ed.), _Calendars and
   Years: Astronomy and Time in the Ancient Near East_, 115-132.
   Oxford: Oxbow.
   - CHECK: Britton is credited with the claim that Nisanu 1 on or
     after the vernal equinox was deliberate. Does he state the EARLY
     rule as a full-moon (Nisanu 15) bound? Does he plot Nisanu 1 minus
     equinox over time (if so, our era table replicates his figure)?
     What does he date the transition to, and does he tie breaches to
     catch-up Ululu IIs?
   - Access: Oxbow volume, no open copy known; ILL or ask an author.

2. **Stern, S. 2012.** _Calendars in Antiquity: Empires, States, and
   Societies_, ch. 2 (The Babylonian calendar). Oxford: OUP.
   - CHECK: Stern's assessment of intentionality claims; his treatment
     of the barley-harvest (agricultural) alternative, which any paper
     must engage; whether he discusses a full-moon/equinox rule for the
     Neo-Babylonian period (also in connection with the later Jewish
     "rule of the equinox").
   - Access: OUP; university library or chapter via ILL.

## Essential - method and framework

3. **Neugebauer, O. 1947.** "A Table of Solstices from Uruk," _JCS_ 1,
   143-148; and **1948.** "Solstices and Equinoxes in Babylonian
   Astronomy during the Seleucid Period," _JCS_ 2, 209-222.
   - CHECK: the Uruk scheme parameters we implemented (anchor, 11;3,10
     tithi increment, 3-month-3-tithi season steps) and his measurement
     of the scheme's lateness against the true equinox (we find +4 to
     +6 days; compare his figures).
   - Access: JSTOR.

4. **Ossendrijver, M. 2018.** "Babylonian Scholarship and the Calendar
   during the Reign of Xerxes." In C. Waerzeggers and M. Seire (eds.),
   _Xerxes and Babylonia: The Cuneiform Evidence_, 135-163.
   - CHECK: his intercalation sequence for 484 BCE onward against our
     tables and against the ten 1956-revision candidates in
     `collation_1956.md` (especially Xerxes 4 vs 5); his dating of
     cycle regularization (we find the full pattern locks at 383 BCE).
   - Access: open PDF circulates (OLA 277 volume; academia.edu).

5. **Parker, R. A., and W. H. Dubberstein. 1956.** _Babylonian
   Chronology 626 B.C.-A.D. 75._ Providence: Brown University Press
   (reprinted 1971).
   - CHECK: the ten intercalation differences and the day-column
     question in `collation_1956.md`; the preface, for whether the
     tables were recomputed after 1942.
   - Access: archive.org lending (babylonianchrono0000park); borrow
     and photograph the relevant pages.

## Supporting

6. **Steele, J. M., and J. M. K. Gray. 2007.** "Studies on Babylonian
   Goal-Year Astronomy II: The Babylonian Calendar and Goal-Year
   Methods of Prediction," _AHES_ 61, 553-600.
   - CHECK: observed vs computed month lengths (calibrates how much the
     real calendar deviated from any visibility computation - bears on
     our day-level error budget).

7. **Steele, J. M. 2007** (ed. volume above) and his "The Length of the
   Month in Mesopotamian Calendars of the First Millennium BC."
   - CHECK: month-length statistics; the 30-day cap behavior.

8. **Britton, J. P. 2002.** "Treatments of Annual Phenomena in
   Cuneiform Sources." In J. M. Steele and A. Imhausen (eds.), _Under
   One Sky_, 21-78.
   - CHECK: irregular cycle use under Cyrus and Cambyses (bears on our
     "transition is at Xerxes, not Cyrus" finding).

9. **Csabai, Z. 2020.** "Intercalary Months and Interest-bearing Loans
   in Babylonia," _Hungarian Assyriological Review_ 1, 61-73. Open
   access. Already used here for intercalary-month placement and
   terminology.

## People to approach (small, approachable field)

- **John Steele** (Brown University) - the central figure for
  Babylonian calendars and astronomy; edited the Britton volume.
- **Mathieu Ossendrijver** (FU Berlin) - Achaemenid-era calendar and
  Babylonian mathematical astronomy.
- **Sacha Stern** (UCL) - calendars in antiquity, intentionality
  debates.
- **Megan Lewis and Joshua Bowen** (Digital Hammurabi) - Assyriologists
  and science communicators; their scholarly focus is Old
  Babylonian/Sumerian rather than Late Babylonian astronomy, but they
  are approachable for informal review, contacts, and outreach.
- **Sean Redmond** - upstream transcription author; owed a courtesy
  note and the five corrections as an issue or PR.

## Order of operations

1. Read items 4 and 9 (open access) and borrow item 5; resolve the
   collation checklist.
2. Obtain items 1-3 via library or author request; map exactly which
   of our claims are replication and which are new.
3. Only then draft the paper and write to the people above.
